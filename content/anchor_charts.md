# Reading Comprehension — Anchor Charts

Seven kid-friendly anchor charts for the Reading Comprehension skills listed in the study guide. Each chart has: a one-line **definition**, **Look for…** clues, **Ask yourself…** questions, and a tiny **example**.

---

## 1. Ideas and Support
**Definition:** A main idea is what a paragraph or text is mostly about. **Support** is the details, facts, examples, or reasons that prove it.

**Look for:**
- A sentence that says the big point (often near the start or end).
- Details, numbers, names, examples that explain the big point.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is this paragraph mostly telling me?
- Which sentences give me proof?

**Example 1:** *Bats are helpful animals.* (idea) *They eat mosquitoes, they pollinate flowers, and they spread seeds.* (support)

**Example 2:** *Recycling is good for the planet.* (idea) *It saves trees, uses less energy than making new things, and keeps trash out of the ocean.* (support)

---

## 2. Text Structure
**Definition:** How an author organizes the information in a text.

**Five common structures:**
| Structure | Signal words |
|---|---|
| Description | for example, such as, also |
| Sequence / Chronological | first, next, then, finally, after |
| Compare & Contrast | like, unlike, both, however, while |
| Cause & Effect | because, so, since, as a result |
| Problem & Solution | problem, solved, answer, fixed |

**Ask yourself:** How is this text put together? What signal words do I see?

**Example 1 (Sequence):** *First, mix the flour and sugar. Next, add the eggs. Then, pour the batter into a pan. Finally, bake for 30 minutes.*

**Example 2 (Cause & Effect):** *The road was icy, so the cars drove very slowly. Because of the cold, several drivers slid into the ditch.*

---

## 3. Figurative Language
**Definition:** Words that mean something more than what they literally say. Authors use them to paint pictures.

| Type | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Simile | compares using *like* or *as* | *Her smile was like sunshine.* |
| Metaphor | says one thing IS another | *The classroom was a zoo.* |
| Personification | gives human traits to things | *The wind whispered.* |
| Hyperbole | huge exaggeration | *I've told you a million times!* |
| Idiom | a saying with a hidden meaning | *It's raining cats and dogs.* |

**Ask yourself:** Does this really mean what it says, or is the author painting a picture?

**Example 1 (Simile):** *The snow was as white as a fresh sheet of paper, and the trees stood like silent guards along the road.*

**Example 2 (Personification + Hyperbole):** *The old door groaned when I pushed it open, and the room was so dusty I thought I would sneeze a thousand times.*

---

## 4. Central Idea
**Definition:** The most important point of the **whole text** (not just one paragraph). It's bigger than the topic.

- **Topic** = one or two words (e.g., *bats*).
- **Central idea** = a full sentence (e.g., *Bats help the environment in many ways.*).

**Ask yourself:**
- What is the whole text mostly about?
- What does the author most want me to understand?

**Tip:** Look at the title, the first paragraph, the last paragraph, and any repeated ideas.

**Example 1:** Topic = *honeybees*. Central idea = *Honeybees do important jobs that help farmers grow food.*

**Example 2:** Topic = *the rainforest*. Central idea = *Rainforests are home to thousands of plants and animals that we cannot find anywhere else.*

---

## 5. Text and Graphic Features
**Definition:** Special parts of a text that help readers find and understand information.

**Look for:**
- **Title / Heading** — tells what a section is about.
- **Bold / italic words** — important vocabulary.
- **Captions** — explain pictures.
- **Maps, diagrams, charts, graphs** — show information visually.
- **Photos / illustrations** — give clues about meaning.
- **Sidebars / fact boxes** — extra information.

**Ask yourself:** What does this feature show me that the words alone don't?

**Example 1:** A science book about volcanoes has a **diagram** with arrows showing where the lava, ash, and gas come from. The diagram lets you SEE the inside of the volcano without reading a long paragraph.

**Example 2:** A book about animals has a **photo** of a sloth with a **caption** that says, *"A sloth can sleep up to 20 hours a day."* The caption gives a fact you might miss in the main text.

---

## 6. Summary
**Definition:** A short retelling of a text in your own words, with only the most important ideas.

**Rules of a good summary:**
1. **Short** — much shorter than the original.
2. **Your own words** — don't copy sentences.
3. **Most important ideas only** — skip small details.
4. **No opinions** — just what the text says.
5. **In order** — keep the events in the right sequence.

**Try this:** *Somebody / Wanted / But / So / Then* for stories.
**Or:** *Topic + Central Idea + 2–3 key supports* for nonfiction.

**Example 1 (Story summary):** *Somebody:* A young girl named Mia. *Wanted:* to win the school spelling bee. *But:* she got nervous and forgot a word in practice. *So:* she practiced every night with her dad. *Then:* she came in second place and felt proud of how hard she had tried.

**Example 2 (Nonfiction summary):** *Honeybees are very important insects. They help farmers by pollinating flowers, they make honey we can eat, and their hives are run by a queen and thousands of worker bees.*

---

## 7. Theme
**Definition:** The **lesson or message** the author wants readers to take away from a story. It's about life, not just about the characters.

**Look for:**
- What problem the main character faces.
- What the character learns by the end.
- How the character changes.

**Ask yourself:**
- What did the character learn?
- What lesson can I learn from this story?

**Common themes:** *Friendship matters. Never give up. Be yourself. Honesty is important. Family helps you grow.*

**Tip:** A theme is a full sentence, not one word. Not "friendship" — *"True friends help each other even when it's hard."*

**Example 1:** In a story where a boy is afraid to try out for the soccer team, but tries anyway and learns he's better than he thought — the theme is *"You won't know what you can do until you try."*

**Example 2:** In a story where two sisters fight over a toy, then realize they had more fun playing together than alone — the theme is *"Sharing with people you love is better than having something all to yourself."*
